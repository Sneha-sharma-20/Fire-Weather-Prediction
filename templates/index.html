What is FWI?

The Forest Weather Index (FWI) is a numerical indicator used to estimate forest fire danger based on weather conditions and fuel moisture levels.

The Fire Weather Index (FWI) is a comprehensive and internationally recognized system used to evaluate and quantify the potential risk and intensity of forest fires based solely on weather conditions. Developed to support fire management and disaster preparedness, the FWI integrates critical meteorological parameters such as air temperature, relative humidity, wind speed, and accumulated rainfall to assess how conducive the environment is to wildfire ignition and spread. Rather than directly predicting the occurrence of fires, the FWI estimates the dryness of forest fuels and the likely behavior of a fire if ignition occurs.

The system is structured into multiple components that represent different layers of forest fuel moisture and fire dynamics. These components collectively capture short-term and long-term drying trends, surface fuel flammability, and deeper organic layer conditions. By combining these elements, the FWI produces a final numerical index that reflects the potential fire intensity and difficulty of fire control. Higher FWI values indicate more severe fire weather conditions, often associated with rapid fire spread, increased flame intensity, and greater challenges for suppression efforts.

Due to its reliability and simplicity, the FWI is widely adopted by meteorological agencies, forestry departments, and disaster management authorities across the world. It plays a crucial role in early warning systems, strategic resource allocation, and policy planning related to wildfire prevention and response. In research and predictive modeling, the FWI also serves as an important target variable, enabling the development of data-driven tools that support proactive forest fire risk assessment and environmental safety planning.

Features affecting Fire

      Core Meteorological Input Features

      These are the primary weather parameters required to compute the Fire Weather Index (FWI) system.
    • 1. Temperature (°C)
      Temperature represents the ambient air temperature near the forest surface. Higher temperatures accelerate the drying of forest fuels, making them more combustible and increasing the likelihood of fire ignition and spread.
    • 2. Relative Humidity (RH %)
      Relative Humidity measures the amount of moisture present in the air compared to the maximum it can hold at a given temperature. Low relative humidity leads to drier fuels, increasing flammability and fire risk.
    • 3. Wind Speed (km/h)
      Wind speed indicates the horizontal movement of air near the ground. Stronger winds supply oxygen to fires, increase flame length, and significantly enhance the rate and direction of fire spread.
    • 4. Rainfall (mm)
      Rainfall represents the amount of precipitation received over a given period. Rain reduces fire danger by increasing moisture content in forest fuels and slowing down fuel drying processes.

      Moisture Codes (Intermediate Indices)

      These indices describe the moisture content of different forest fuel layers.
    • 5. FFMC - Fine Fuel Moisture Code
      FFMC represents the moisture content of surface-level fine fuels such as dry leaves, grass, pine needles, and small twigs. It reflects short-term drying and wetting effects and is highly sensitive to daily weather changes. Higher FFMC values indicate very dry surface fuels that ignite easily.
    • 6. DMC - Duff Moisture Code
      DMC measures the moisture content of moderately deep organic layers (loosely compacted forest floor material). It reflects medium-term drying trends influenced by temperature and rainfall over several days to weeks. Higher DMC values indicate drier conditions and increased fire sustainability.
    • 7. DC - Drought Code
      DC represents the moisture content of deep, compact organic soil layers. It reflects long-term drying effects caused by extended periods of dry weather and drought. Higher DC values indicate severe drought conditions and the potential for deep-burning fires.

      Fire Behavior Indices

      These indices describe how a fire is likely to behave once ignited.
    • 8. ISI - Initial Spread Index
      ISI combines the effects of wind speed and FFMC to estimate the expected rate of fire spread immediately after ignition. Higher ISI values indicate faster-spreading fires that are harder to control.
    • 9. BUI - Buildup Index
      BUI represents the total amount of fuel available for combustion by combining DMC and DC. It reflects the overall dryness and quantity of burnable material in the forest. Higher BUI values indicate more fuel accumulation and greater fire intensity potential.

How the Fire Weather Index (FWI) Is Calculated

The Fire Weather Index (FWI) is calculated through a multi-stage process that combines meteorological inputs with fuel moisture and fire behavior indices. The system does not directly predict fire occurrence; instead, it estimates potential fire intensity based on weather-driven fuel conditions.

Step 1: Collection of Meteorological Inputs

The calculation begins with the collection of daily weather observations, which include air temperature, relative humidity, wind speed, and rainfall. These variables influence the drying and wetting of forest fuels and form the foundation of the FWI system.

Step 2: Computation of Fuel Moisture Codes

Using the meteorological inputs, three fuel moisture codes are calculated:

  • Fine Fuel Moisture Code (FFMC): Estimates the moisture content of surface-level fine fuels and responds rapidly to daily weather changes.
  • Duff Moisture Code (DMC): Represents the moisture content of moderately deep organic layers and reflects medium-term drying trends.
  • Drought Code (DC): Indicates the moisture content of deep soil layers and captures long-term drought effects.

Step 3: Calculation of Fire Behavior Indices

The fuel moisture codes are then combined with wind speed to estimate fire behavior:

  • Initial Spread Index (ISI): Calculated using wind speed and FFMC, it estimates the expected rate of fire spread immediately after ignition.
  • Buildup Index (BUI): Derived from DMC and DC, it represents the total amount of fuel available for combustion.

Step 4: Final Fire Weather Index (FWI) Calculation

The final FWI value is computed by combining the Initial Spread Index (ISI) and the Buildup Index (BUI). This value represents the potential intensity of a fire under current weather conditions. Higher FWI values indicate more severe fire danger, faster fire spread, and increased difficulty in fire suppression.

Summary

In summary, the FWI calculation follows a hierarchical structure: meteorological inputs influence fuel moisture codes, which determine fire behavior indices, ultimately producing a single Fire Weather Index value that reflects overall fire danger.
